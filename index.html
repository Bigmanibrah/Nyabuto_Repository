Tool 1: The River Code Play Video

THE TOOL WAS ADOPTED FROM THE MINISTRY OF AGRICULTURE, KENYA. NAVCDP PROJECT. PARTICIPATORY INTEGRATED COMMUNITY DEVELOPMENT PROCESS IN MAY, 2024

OBSERVATION

I observed that all the members within the society in different positions and serve different purposes, which necessitates coexistence between the members of the society. The two members of the group who were carried across the flooded river had only one goal of crossing the river to their destination. The remaining lady and the gentleman had a visionary mindset and curiosity of wanting to be shown on how to reach the other side of the river and if there's need of them coming they will not help to navigate the flooded river.

LESSONS LEARNT

The lesson I learnt from the river code play is the society has people with long term vision and short term vision. For instance the two gentlemen who were carried across the river only wanted to get to the other side of the river, the lady on the other hand had a long vision by requesting to be shown on how to cross the river rather being carried across the river.

Tool 2: Take a Step Video

Adopted from the Ministry of Agriculture. NAVCDP Project. PICD Training, May 2024

OBSERVATION

During the role-play exercise, clear differences in wealth and opportunity among the characters became visible. Some participants moved forward quickly with almost every statement, while others remained in the same position or only took a few steps.

The characters representing financially stable individuals such as the businessman, the retired civil servant, and the local pastor were able to take many steps. They could afford basic needs like food and healthcare, as well as luxuries such as holidays, vehicles, and higher education for their children.

On the other hand, vulnerable individuals such as the widow with six children, the orphaned children, the drunkard, and the casual laborer barely moved. These characters struggled to meet even basic needs like balanced meals, medical care, and education.

It was also observed that family size and dependency level had a significant impact. Even individuals with some income, like teachers, were limited in how far they could move due to having many dependants or unemployed spouses.

By the end of the exercise, participants were spread out across the field, forming a visible gap between the rich, the better-off, the poor, and the very poor. This physical distance clearly represented inequality within a community.

LESSON LEARNT

Inequality in society is very real and often influenced by factors such as income, employment, family size, and social support systems. Not everyone starts from the same position, and this affects their ability to access opportunities.

The exercise also highlighted how vulnerable groups such as widows, orphans, and the unemployed face multiple challenges that limit their progress. Without support, they are likely to remain trapped in poverty.

The demonstration showed the importance of education, stable employment, and investment in improving one's economic status. Characters with steady income sources or assets were able to progress further.

The activity emphasized the need for community support and inclusive development programs. To reduce inequality, interventions should focus on uplifting the most vulnerable groups through education, financial support, and access to basic services.

Tool 3: The Sinking Boat

Adopted from NAVCDP PICD Process

OBSERVATION

During the role-play, it was evident that decision-making in a crisis situation is often influenced by perceived importance and social roles rather than fairness or equality. As the boat began to sink, all participants initially agreed to sacrifice their luggage, showing a willingness to give up material possessions for survival.

However, when the situation worsened and people had to be thrown overboard, tension and self-interest increased. Each character tried to justify their survival based on their role in society. For example, the doctor emphasized saving lives, the farmer highlighted food production, the policeman focused on security, and the headmaster stressed education and leadership.

It was also noticeable that the final survivors (the doctor, businessman, headmaster, and coxswain) represented positions of authority, economic power, or control over the situation. This reflected how power dynamics can influence who gets prioritized during crises.

LESSONS LEARNT

People tend to prioritize their own survival and justify it using their social roles or perceived importance.

The activity also showed that society often places different values on different roles. While every role is important in reality, some are seen as more "valuable" than others, which can lead to unequal treatment.

The student and the woman with a child represented the future and caregiving roles, yet they were still sacrificed. This highlights how vulnerable individuals can be overlooked even when they are important to society.

The demonstration encouraged critical thinking about fairness, ethics, and decision-making. It raises important questions about who should be prioritized in emergencies and whether such decisions should be based on social roles, equality, or moral principles.

Tool 4: Acres of Diamonds

Adopted from the Ministry of Agriculture Kenya NAVCDP Project PICD Process, May 2024

OBSERVATION

During the narration of the story, it became clear how easily people can overlook the value of what they already have while chasing distant opportunities. Hemed started as a wealthy and contented man, but the moment he heard about diamonds, his mindset changed. He became dissatisfied and began to believe that true wealth existed elsewhere.

The imagery of his journey from his rich farm to distant lands like the mountains, Taita Hills, and eventually the shores of Kilifi—highlighted the desperation and exhaustion that came with his search. As he moved further away from home, his condition worsened, showing how blind pursuit of unknown opportunities can lead to loss rather than gain.

At the same time, the discovery made by the new owner of Hemed's farm was striking. The diamonds were always present in the very land Hemed had abandoned. This contrast between Hemed's tragic end and the new owner's unexpected fortune emphasized the irony of the situation.

The story also captured strong emotions hope, greed, frustration, and regret. Hemed's transformation from contentment to despair illustrated how quickly one's perspective can change when influenced by external ideas of wealth.

LESSON LEARNT

The story also teaches the importance of contentment. Hemed's downfall began when he became dissatisfied with his life. This shows that lack of contentment can lead to poor decisions and unnecessary risks.

The demonstration further highlights the value of awareness and perspective. The new owner succeeded not because he searched far, but because he paid attention to his surroundings. This shows that success often comes from observation, patience, and making the most of available resources.

Finally, the story emphasizes self-reliance and personal growth. The idea of "acres of diamonds" symbolizes the potential within ourselves, our skills, talents, environment, and opportunities. When we invest in these, we can achieve meaningful and sustainable success.

Tool 5: The 65-Year Old Couple

OBSERVATION

During the narration and discussion of the story, it was evident that the couple demonstrated strong commitment, patience, and faith throughout their marriage. Despite facing pressure from family members and the disappointment of not having children for many years, they remained loyal to each other and did not allow external influence to break their union.

The man’s refusal to marry a second wife showed deep respect and love for his wife, as well as a strong sense of personal values. Even when they reached an age where having children seemed biologically impossible, the couple continued to hope and pray, showing resilience and belief in a higher power.

When they were finally given the opportunity to have a child at an old age, the condition that they must agree on the sex of the child introduced an element of decision-making and unity. It highlighted the importance of communication and mutual agreement within a relationship.

The story also created curiosity and reflection among participants, especially on what choice the couple might make and why, revealing different perspectives and preferences among individuals.

LESSONS LEARNT

One key lesson from the story is the importance of patience and perseverance. The couple waited for many years without losing hope, showing that some outcomes in life require time and faith.

Another important lesson is the value of loyalty and commitment in relationships. Despite societal and family pressure, the man chose to remain faithful to his wife, demonstrating integrity and respect in marriage.

The story also highlights the importance of unity and joint decision-making. When faced with the choice of the child’s sex, the couple had to agree together, showing that successful relationships require communication, compromise, and mutual understanding.

The demonstration teaches that external pressure should not dictate personal decisions. The couple remained true to their beliefs and values rather than conforming to expectations from others.

The story emphasizes faith and hope. Even when circumstances seemed impossible, the couple continued to believe, reminding us that maintaining a positive outlook can be powerful in overcoming life’s challenges.

Tool 6: Secret in the Box

OBSERVATION

During the activity, it became clear that the level of access to information greatly influenced how accurately each participant could describe the contents of the box.

The widely travelled person, who only shook the closed box, relied on assumptions and guesses based on sound. As a result, their answers were mostly inaccurate or incomplete. This showed that experience alone is not always enough when there is limited information.

The moderately travelled person, who was blindfolded but allowed to touch the items, gave better descriptions than the first participant. Through touch, they could identify shapes and textures, but still missed important details such as color, labels, or exact identity of the items.

The least travelled (local) person, who could see, touch, and smell the contents, gave the most accurate and detailed description. They were even able to read labels and consult others, making their responses more comprehensive and correct.

LESSONS LEARNT

One key lesson is that access to complete and accurate information is more important than assumptions or past experience. The person with full access to the box (seeing, touching, and smelling) was able to give the most reliable answers.

Another lesson is the importance of using multiple senses and sources of information when making decisions. Relying on only one method (like hearing or touch) can lead to incomplete or incorrect conclusions.

The demonstration also highlighted the danger of making assumptions without evidence. The widely travelled person made guesses based on limited clues, which resulted in many errors.

The exercise emphasized that knowledge is situational. Being "exposed" or experienced does not automatically guarantee accurate judgments. What matters most is having the right information and using appropriate methods to analyze it.
